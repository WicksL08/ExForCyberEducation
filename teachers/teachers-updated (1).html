How to Get Students Ready

🗣️ Opening Discussion Prompts

Start with these questions before Session 1 to activate prior knowledge and build curiosity:

  • "Have you ever wondered how hackers actually break into systems?"
  • "What information about yourself do you think is publicly available online?"
  • "What makes a password strong — and why does it matter?"
  • "If you could design a cybersecurity challenge, what would it look like?"

🧊 Ice Breaker — "What's Your Digital Footprint?"

Ask students to spend 2 minutes writing down every app, website, or platform they used in the last 24 hours. Then discuss: Who can see that information? What could someone figure out about you from that list? This sets up the OSINT module perfectly and gets students thinking like analysts from day one.

📋 Suggested Classroom Setup

  • One device per student (laptop or Chromebook preferred)
  • Students seated in pairs or small groups of 2–3 to encourage collaboration
  • Display the CTFd portal URL and Learning Lab link on the board at the start of each session
  • Reserve the first 10 minutes of each session for the mini-lesson before opening CTFd
  • Reserve the last 5 minutes for a whole-class debrief or reflection prompt

💬 What to Tell Students & Parents

Use these talking points when introducing the study:

  • "This is a real university research study from The University of Alabama — your participation helps improve cybersecurity education statewide."
  • "All data is completely anonymous — no names, no grades, nothing tied to you personally."
  • "A CTF is like a cybersecurity scavenger hunt — you solve puzzles to find hidden flags and earn points."
  • "Participation is 100% voluntary — you can stop at any time."

📝 Mini-Lesson Talking Points by Module

Session 1 — OSINT: Explain that OSINT stands for Open-Source Intelligence. Analysts use only publicly available information — no hacking required. Key point: everything posted online leaves a trace.

Session 2 — Cryptography: Introduce the idea that humans have been hiding messages for thousands of years. Show a simple Caesar cipher on the board and let students decode a word by hand before going to CTFd.

Session 3 — Password Security: Ask: "How does a website know your password without storing it?" Introduce hashing as a one-way lock. Demo how adding salt changes the hash completely.

⏱️ Suggested Session Timeline

  • 0–5 min — Review consent forms and pre-test status (Session 1 only)
  • 5–15 min — Mini-lesson: introduce the module topic with discussion
  • 15–45 min — Students work through CTF challenges independently or in pairs
  • 45–50 min — Whole-class debrief: what did you find? What surprised you?
  • Final session — Reserve last 10 min for post-test and feedback forms
